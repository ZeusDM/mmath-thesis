\section*{Structure of the thesis}

The remainder of this thesis is organized into four chapters.

\Cref{chap:polynomials} introduces Macdonald polynomials from an algebraic perspective.
We define several variations of Macdonald polynomials including the modified Macdonald polynomials $\tilde{H}_\mu$, the integral form symmetric Macdonald polynomials $J_\mu$, the symmetric Macdonald polynomials $P_\mu$, and the permuted-basement Macdonald polynomials $E_\alpha^\sigma$.
Readers interested primarily in the combinatorial aspects may safely skip this chapter, as alternate combinatorial descriptions of these polynomials are provided in \Cref{chap:models}.

\Cref{chap:models} introduces a combinatorial framework for studying Macdonald polynomials.  
The central objects are fillings, considered in several variations.  
Each version of the Macdonald polynomials defined in \Cref{chap:polynomials} is then realized as a weighted generating function over an appropriate class of fillings.
The weights are expressed in terms of two key statistics on fillings: the major index (\(\maj\)) and the inversion number (\(\inv\)).

\Cref{chap:bijective} presents bijective results for Macdonald polynomials.
On the first part of this chapter, following the work of \cite{KLO22}, we describe the bijection between fillings $\Fill(\mu)$ and $\Fill(s_i \cdot \mu)$ that preserves the statistics $\inv$ and $\maj$.
This bijection implies that $\tilde{H}_\mu(\mathbf{x};q,t) = \tilde{H}_{s_i \cdot \mu}(\mathbf{x};q,t)$, and by repeated application shows that the modified Macdonald polynomials are invariant under rearrangements of the partition.
On the second part of this chapter, we adapt the bijection to the setting of permuted-basement Macdonald polynomials $E_\alpha^\sigma(\mathbf{x};q,t)$, to obtain an identity for these polynomials.

\Cref{chap:probabilistic} extends the bijective methods to a probabilistic setting.
We introduce the concept of probabilistic bijections as a generalization of weight-preserving bijections, which can also be used to prove identities between generating functions.
We explore their applications to Macdonald polynomials by proving that \(E_\alpha^\sigma(\mathbf{x};q,t) = E_\alpha^{\sigma s_i}(\mathbf{x};q,t)\) when $\alpha_i = \alpha_{i+1}$.
